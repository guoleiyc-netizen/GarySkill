# Material Collection Search Strategies

This document provides best practices and strategies for effective material collection through web search during Stage 2-3 of the Super Writer workflow.

## Core Principles

### 1. Information Type Classification

Before searching, identify what type of information is needed:

**Data & Statistics:**
- Official sources: Government, industry associations
- Research firms: Gartner, Forrester, McKinsey
- Academic institutions
- Company reports (earnings, annual reports)

**Trends & Insights:**
- Industry publications and trade journals
- Thought leader blogs and LinkedIn posts
- Conference proceedings
- Recent news articles

**Cases & Examples:**
- Case study databases
- Customer success stories
- Industry examples and benchmarks
- "Best practices" compilations

**Background & Context:**
- Wikipedia for overview
- Industry encyclopedias
- Educational resources
- Historical analyses

**Expert Opinions:**
- Thought leader content
- Expert interviews
- Podcast transcripts
- Panel discussions

## Keyword Strategy

### Basic Principles

**Specific vs. Broad Balance:**
- Start broad to understand landscape
- Narrow with specific terms for depth
- Use 2-4 words per query typically
- Avoid overly complex queries

**Chinese vs. English Coordination:**

**When to search BOTH languages:**
- International brands/companies (Tesla, Apple, Amazon)
- Global trends and technologies (AI, blockchain, metaverse)
- Cross-cultural topics (marketing, business practices)
- International markets and industries

**When to search CHINESE only:**
- Domestic Chinese companies and brands
- China-specific policies and regulations
- Local market data and trends
- Chinese cultural topics

**When to search ENGLISH only:**
- Western brands without Chinese presence
- English-language content sources
- International academic research
- Global but not China-relevant topics

## Source Quality Assessment

### Hierarchy of Source Quality

**Tier 1 - Highest Quality (Prefer These):**
- Original research and data
- Official company/government sources
- Peer-reviewed academic papers
- Reputable research firms (Gartner, McKinsey, etc.)
- Major industry publications

**Tier 2 - Good Quality (Use with Confidence):**
- Established news outlets (not tabloids)
- Industry expert blogs
- Professional associations
- Company blogs (for own info)
- Well-known thought leaders

**Tier 3 - Use with Caution:**
- General blogs and websites
- Social media posts (except verified experts)
- Forum discussions
- Anonymous sources
- Aggregate content sites

**Tier 4 - Avoid or Verify:**
- Content farms
- Unverified claims
- Clearly biased sources
- Spam or promotional-only sites
- Very outdated information (> 5 years for trends)

### Quality Indicators

**Positive Signals:**
- Author credentials visible
- Publication date recent (for trends)
- Citations and references provided
- Professional presentation
- No excessive ads/spam
- Domain authority (established site)

**Red Flags:**
- No author or date
- Excessive spelling/grammar errors
- Clickbait headlines
- Too many ads
- Dubious claims without evidence
- Poor site design/professionalism

## Dynamic Adjustment Strategies

### When to Add More Searches

**Insufficient Information:**
- Initial searches yield little relevant content
- Information is too general or vague
- Missing key perspectives or data
- Conflicting information needs verification

**Action:** Add 1-2 targeted searches with refined keywords

**New Angle Discovered:**
- Search results reveal unexpected valuable angle
- Realize need for additional context
- Discover related subtopic worth exploring

**Action:** Pivot search strategy, add relevant searches

**Quality Issues:**
- Sources found are low quality
- Information is outdated
- Credibility is questionable

**Action:** Search with more specific, authoritative keywords

### When to Stop Searching

**Sufficient Signals:**
- âœ… Have 3-5 solid sources
- âœ… Key data points collected
- âœ… Multiple perspectives obtained
- âœ… Information starts repeating
- âœ… Can answer user's core question
- âœ… Diminishing returns on new searches

**Don't Over-Search:**
- âŒ Going beyond 8-10 searches without clear need
- âŒ Searching just to fill arbitrary number
- âŒ Finding redundant information
- âŒ Getting distracted by tangential topics

## Special Scenarios

### Limited or No Search Results

**If search yields poor results:**

1. **Broaden keywords** - Remove specific terms
2. **Try alternative phrasing** - Different words, same concept
3. **Search in other language** - Chinese if searched English, vice versa
4. **Look for related topics** - Adjacent or parent categories
5. **Use more general industry terms** - Step back from specifics

**If truly nothing found:**
- Inform user honestly
- Offer to create based on general knowledge
- Suggest user provide materials

### Time-Sensitive or Breaking Topics

**For very recent events/trends:**
- Include "2025" or current month/year in search
- Use "latest" or "recent" modifiers
- Search news sources specifically
- Check official announcements first

### Niche or Specialized Topics

**For obscure or highly specialized:**
- Start with broader category search
- Find industry-specific sources first
- Look for expert communities (Reddit, specialized forums)
- Academic sources may be more relevant
- LinkedIn posts from practitioners

## Integration with Writing Task

### Aligning Search with Methodology

**If planning to use AIDA Model:**
- Search for persuasive examples
- Look for conversion data
- Find emotional trigger research

**If planning to use Storytelling:**
- Search for narrative examples
- Look for character development tips
- Find story structure analysis

**If planning to use W.R.I.T.E:**
- Search for systematic content processes
- Look for research-backed approaches
- Find structured examples

**General Principle:** Search strategy should support selected methodology
